Notes for Sir Edwin Sandys Kt
2157 statesman and colonist. Called first legislative assembly helo in the colonies, at Jamestown 1619. BA Corpus Christi Oxford 1579. MP 1586 for Andover, then Plympton 1588-9 and 1592-3. Travelled extensively. Returned to England in 1599 and entered the service of King James in Scotland and came with im to ENgland when he succeeded Elizabeth in 1603. Kinghted by James in 1603. Became member of Countil of VA 1607. With Sir Francis Bacon drew up remonstrance against King’s conduct against the parliament during session of 1604-11. Began to drift away from the court party. Was granted lands by King in Kent. MP Hinton 1614. Professed loyalty to the King but opposed his conception of absolutisn, and formulated the doctrine of what later became the late Whig dogma, that the origin of monarchy lay in election, and that the duties of the monarch and his subjects were reciprocal. James was incensed and Sandys was summoned before the privy council upon the dissolution of parliament 1614 and gave bond not ot leave London without permission but the case was not presssed.

Between 1614-1621, when there was no parliament, he became a member of the East India Company bef 1614 and served on its committee 1619-23 and 1625-29. He joined the Somers Island, or Bermuda Company 1615. This company was later merged with the VA Co and the Sandys tribe of Bermuda derived its name from him. He took over as treasurer of CEO of VA Co in 1617. He was sympathetic to the settling of the Leyden Puritans in VA. He was convinced that for the colony to prosper the colonists needed a large measure of self govmt. The VAC to which upwards of 100 MPs belonged became the forum for discussion of political matters between 1614 adn 1621 when no Parliament was called. Sandys was looked upon by the king as hthe leader of the opposition and his greatest enemy. The King tried to prevent the election of Sandys in 1620, but he was succeede byt he Earl of Southampton, his friend. Imprisoned briefly in 1621.

In Oct 1623, King James brought suit agains t VAC to annul its charter, and notwithstanding oppositino, the court declared the charter null and void jul 24 1624 and VA became henceforth a royal colony.

Sandys continued to be interested in the East Indies Co and continued in parliament.

Married four times (1) Margared, dau of John Everleigh of Devan (2) Anne dau of THomas Southcoat, (3) Elizabeth day of Thomas NEvinson, (4) Catherine dau of Sir Richard Bulkeley. By (4) he had dau Mary who m RIchard, 3rd son of Robert, Lord Spencer, later Duke of Marlborough.

Biography
One of the most brilliant parliamentary figures of his generation, Sandys burst suddenly and unexpectedly onto the parliamentary stage in 1604 at the relatively advanced age of 42, establishing a dominance so complete that Prestwich has dubbed him ‘the uncrowned king of the Commons in James’s reign’. This was, perhaps, an exaggeration, but even Gardiner admitted that, apart from Bacon, ‘no man enjoyed the confidence of the House more than Sir Edwin Sandys’.21 His greatest strengths were his razor-sharp intellect and an ability to articulate clearly and powerfully the concerns of his colleagues. Although Notestein sourly remarked that he ‘was not gifted with words’,22 Robert Bowyer*, Sir William Strode* and lord chancellor Ellesmere (Thomas Egertonâ€ ) all praised Sandys’s eloquence, while Tobie Matthew* remarked upon his ‘flowing speech’.23 Even Nathaniel Boteler, an enemy in colonial matters, considered him ‘a great speaker’.24 Perhaps only Sir Robert Phelips, Sandys’s colleague in five parliaments, was more widely admired for his oratory. Yet Sandys’s parliamentary career ultimately ended almost as swiftly as it had begun: after July 1625 he was a pariah in the Commons, and by 1628 his reputation had sunk so low that he proved incapable even of finding a seat.
