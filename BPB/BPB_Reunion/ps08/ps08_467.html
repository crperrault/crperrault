Notes for Charles Juchereau De St-Denis De Beaumarchais
[Jette] engageur ouest 1695-1703. lieutenant general civil et criminel Montréal et conseiller du roi 1693-1702. Seigneur de St- Denis a la mort de son frere Joseph.

[DBC] Charles became royal judge of Montréal after the death of his father-in-law who had first held the office and it afforded him a knowledge of the fur trade in which he was to become a leading figure. With his wife and his sister Charlotte Francoise, he made frequent loans to western traders (including to Daupin de La Forest qv, who was to become Charlotte Francoise's husband). Stricter regulation of the trade in 1696 made it necessary for him to obtain leave from his duties as judge to represent the merchants in paris in 1699. He returned the following year to help found the Compagnie du Canada and became receiver of beaver at a salary of 6000 livres, a vast increase over his stipend as judge. He also obtained in 1700 the rights to estabish a tannery on the lower Ohio. This was in an area of great strategic influence -- the Tennessee, Cumberland, Ohio and Mississippi RIvers all flowed together there, with the Wabash and Missouri not far away. Juchereau's establishment would discourage Anglo-French fur trading, and promote Indian alliances. He was permitted to secure any skins for tanning except beaver; he was absent from his udgeship for three years; he coudl mine any deposits of lead or copper. The scheme drew prolonged objections from Juchereau's colleagues in the COmpagnie du Canada who predicted the many abuses he coudl practice, but he was supported by LeMoyne d'Iberville, who had married his niece. Having built a fort at the mouth of the Ohio, he died in 1703, perhaps from an epidemic, and the venture disintegrated. One of the other members of the expedition, Philippe de Mandeville, attempted to salvage the tannery, but many of the accumulated furs were stolen, damaged by moths, or lost to flooding. Juchereau's brother Louis then took over. Had Juchereau been successful, he might have drastically altered the course of the empire west of the Alleghanies.
