Notes for Jean Baptiste Hertel De Rouville
[Jette] chevalier de St-Louis 1721. concession de la seigneurie de Rouville 1694.

[DBC] Took up soldiering at an early age and was taught by his father. In 1704, he led a raid against Deerfield at the head of 250 men, including 200 Abenaki and Iroquois Indians from Caughnawaga to avenge the Abenaki who had just been the victims of a bloody raid. They massacred 54 settlers and took 120 prisonners, including the minister, John Bancroft, whose wife had been killed, and three of his children. Rouville himself was wounded in the raid. Four years later in 1708, Rouville and JB de St-Ours Deschaillons led a similar expedition intended to be against Portsmouth NH, but which ended up being against the small village of Haverhill. The church and sere burned and 15 people were killed, including the minister and his wife.

In 1709, Hertel directed another raid against Deerfield to forestall a possible attack against Montréal. English losses amounted to several dead. Prisonners were taken and attmpts at exchanges were made. For some time, every village in New England was on the alert and Hertel was much feared.

His most impressive exploits had been accomplished against defenceless people and to preserve the goodwill of the Indian allies, he had been constrained to permit the carrying out of acts of barbarity that were unworthy of civilized beings. On the other hand, the psychological effects of these attacks were considerable. It is understandable that this commander, trained in Indian methods of combat, was never favourably reported on by the English chroniclers.
