Un petit carnet, composé de quelques feuillets, retrouvé dans les archives de l’institution, apporte quelques éléments éclairants en ce sens. Ce document intitulé Cahier d’abjurations depuis 1662 jusqu’à 1847 (ASQ P8, 1847) et attribué à Antoine Parant, est en fait une liste manuscrite de noms d’émigrants protestants ayant abjuré leur croyance pour adopter la foi et la religion catholiques. Une petite croix inscrite en marge du nom indique les individus décédés et les quelques annotations permettent de situer plusieurs de ces conversions lors de l’épidémie de choléra de 1832.” ASQ Polygraphie 8, no 66-67, Cahier d’abjuration depuis 1662 jusqu’à 1847, 1847.

[Letter from Peter Gagné, archiviste at Musee de la Civilization de Québec March 7, 2013.]
Hello Mr. Perrault,
I was forwarded your inquiry to the Musée de la civilisation regarding the conversion of Jane Smith, wife of Thomas Reed. A substantial number of e-mail requests, combined with the responsibility of the researchers in the reading room, has caused a greater delay than usual in responding to your request.
Nonetheless, I was able to quickly look through the document that you mentioned, the “Cahier d’abjuration depuis 1662 jusqu’à 1847” (Polygraphie 8, no 66 & Polygraphie 8, no 67), and I think that I may have found your Jane Smith.
For the year 1835, I found “+ Smith, Mary Dme (Reed) Ecos – Q”. I believe that this is the woman you are looking for. Note that the name Mary may be for Mary Jane. The abbreviation Dme (the last 2 letters in superscript) appears to be for “Dame”, indicating that it is a question of a married woman or widow. The name Reed in parentheses apparently refers to her husband’s name. Note that in Quebec, women are usually referred to by their maiden names. The combination of “Dame” (i.e. “madame”) and Reed indicates a woman with the maiden name Smith married to a Reed. “Ecos” appears to be an abbreviation for “Écosse” or “Écossaise” – Scotland or Scottish (female), indicating her origin. The “Q”, which appears almost as a “2” in the text, indicates that the conversion took place at Quebec. The + before the entry, apparently a cross, may indicate that the conversion was prior to her death, although there is no key or legend in the text explaining this.
The register of conversions contains no further information about the people in question and there are no other documents in the archives of the Quebec Seminary pertaining to Jane Smith. I should say that her name does not appear in the index, though there exists the possibility that her name appears in a document that is not indexed, as is the case with the register of conversions. I should note that the seminary archives are not likely to contain documents with genealogical information, like parish registers, as these documents are more likely to be found in the parish or diocesan archives.
I hope that this information helps you, and I wish you luck in your research.
- Peter Gagné, archiviste
