Notes for Guillemette Hébert
Arrive avec ses parents en 1617.

[DBC] b in Paris or Dieppe. She and her husband inherited half of her father's estate on his death in 1627 and Guillaume Couillard administered the estate as Guillaume Hebert, her brother, was still a minor.

She was interested in indian children and often the godmother at their baptisms. She received in her home Charité and Espérance, two of the three indian girls, proteges of Champlain, whom he had hoped to take to France with him. Her household also contained Olivier Le Jeune, a negro boy from Madagascar brought up the river by the English, sold to Olivier Le Baillif, and given by him to the Couillard family. By 1648, the family had 10 children and other servants. She and her husband made many gifts for religious and charitable purposes. She sold to Mgr Laval the land for the petit seminaire. Her disposal of this valuable property met with strong objections from the younger generation and the litigation begun by these prospective heirs was to continue generation after generation even into the 20th century.

Saddened by the dissention in her family, whe withdrew to the convent of the Hotel-Dieu where she spent her last years. She was buried beside her husband in the chapel there. At that time her descendants numbered over 250.
