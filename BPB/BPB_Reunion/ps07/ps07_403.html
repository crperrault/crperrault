Notes for Rev. William Hales
309Professor of Oriental Languages, Trinity College, Dublin; Chancellor of the diocese of Emly. Eminent chronologist whose 22 published works include "De motibus planetarum dissertatio" (1782) and "Analysis Aequationum" (1784).

1037 Educated by his maternal uncle, Rev. James Hingston. Trinity College, Dublin 1764-8. As tutor at the college, he wore a white wig to obviate the objections of parents to his youthful appearance. He occasionally roused students with a dose of cold water. He also held the professorship of oriental languages. His first publications, “Sonorum doctrina rationalis et experimentalis” (1778) and “De motibus Palnetarum dissertatio” (1782) were studies of sound and the motion of planets following Newtonian theory. He published “Analysis Aequationum” in 1784, for which he was complimented by Lagrange. In 1788, he relinquished his professorship and retired to Killeshandra, Co. Cavan, where he became rector; he was said to have been a good parish priest. In 1812, he also held the chancellorship of the diocese of Emly. He was a kind-hearted, well-informed man, who told anecdotes well. His best-known work, “A New Analysis of Chronology”, published by subscription 1809-12, occupied him for 20 years. In it, he argues for investigation from the original sources. He gives a chronology of the entire Bible, and gives a portion of the early history of the world. He suffered from an attack of yellow fever in 1801, from which he recovered, but from about 1820 he suffered from melancholy and his mind seems to have become disordered. He died on 30 Jan 1831 at the age of 83. Hales married, about the middle of 1791, Mary, second dau of Archdeacon Whitty. THey had two sons and two dau. A list of his 22 publications appears in his “Essay on the Origin and Purity of the Primitive Church of the British Isles” (1819). [Works are listed in his DNB entry]
