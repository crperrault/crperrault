Chirurgien aide major des armées du roi, régiment de Berry. Vint en Nouvelle France en 1757 de St Sauveur de Courcy, dioc de Lasu (sp?). Il pansa Montcalm lorsqu'il fut blessé à mort sur les Plaines d'Abraham. Le 13 sept 1759, durant la bataille, il fut capturé par le capt Fraser, et tous deux devinrent d'excellents amis par la suite. Après la conquête, il quitta l'armée et s'établit à Québec comme médecin. Il fut nomme chirurgien des milices canadiennes, puis fut honoré de la commission de chirurgien de la garnison de Québec, en 1776.

[DBC] Badelard came to Louisbourg in 1757 as surgeon-major to the Regiment de Berry. He was shipwrecked on the Toison d'Or on his way to Québec in 1757. He was present at the battle of the Plains of Abraham and taken prisonner by John Fraser.

He served as surgeon to the Canadian militia in the was against the Americans in 1775. In 1776, he was appointed by Gov. Carleton to investigate a disease connected with the presence of Scottish soldiers at Baie St Paul. His "Direction pour la guérison du mal de la baie St-Paul" (1785) was one of the earliest medical texts published in Canada. Badelard remarked that the symptoms were first a throat inflammation and the appearance of pustules on the genitals, then severe pain in the joints, then swelling of the periosteum followed by the general destruction of the victim's bone tissue. He suggested a treatment but did not dare identify the malady, which was identified by Charles Blake, a Montréal surgeon, and F.X. Swediaur, a doctor in Scotland, as a venereal disease. Although the measures he suggested (mercury and diet) were curative, he said almost nothing about prophylaxis. He was also well known as a surgeon, in particular for his skill at cystotomies.

Although well-respected as a specialist, he was not an easy person to get along with. Montcalm complained of this, and his wife left her husband around 1770 to escape from his "bad behaviour" and the "violence" he inflicted on her. It is said that when he died, the parish priest at l'Ancienne Lorette tried to prevent his burial in the parish cemetery.

P.-G. Roy, “Le chirurgien Badelard,” BRH, 2 (1896): 45
