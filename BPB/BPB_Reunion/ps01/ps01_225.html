Prit part aux batailles de Monongahela, Abraham, et Sainte-Foye.

[LBP] indique que Suzanne Rhéaume etait sa 2eme epouse, mais Casgrain n'en mentionne pas d'autre.

[Sulte VIII, 14] Place à la tète d'un commerce de pelleteries très prospère, il parvint en peu de temps à acquérir une forte influence parmi les sauvages. Durant le siège du Détroit (mai-août 1764) il usa de son ascendant sur Pontiac pour protéger les Canadiens et tout en secourant la garnison de la ville, il recevait dans sa maison de campagne le redoutable chef des bandes qui tenaient toute la contrée en alarme. Peu après, il fut nommé surintendant des sauvages. Ayant servi la cause anglaise avec zèle, de 1775 à 1783, il perdit ses nombreuses propriétés situées dans les environs de Détroit. Lorsqu'il s'aperçut que le traité de 1783 englobait une partie de ses terres dans les limites des États-Unis, il traversa la rivière Détroit et, suivi de plusieurs colons, fonda une paroisse dans le township d'Yarmouth (Sandwich) comte d'Essex. Lord Dorchester le nomma juge en 1788. Son influence était générale parmi les Anglais et les Canadiens. M. Baby mourut en 1796, laissant une puissante fortune a ses onze enfants, parmi lesquels trois, Daniel, Antoine et Louis, devinrent officiers dans l'armée anglaise.

[DBC]Jacques's father, Jacques Baby de Ranville, was a scion of decayed nobility of sourhern France and a sergeant in the Regiment de Carignan who married in Canada and settled as a rural merchant and father. His son Raymond got involved in the fur trade. Raymond's wife, Therese Lecomte Dupre was the daughter of a Montréal family deeply involved in the fur trade although of seigneurial rank. Their son followed their traces.

By 1753 he was a trader and indian agent at Chiningué (Logstown, now Ambridge PA). In the Seven Years War he, his brothers Louis and Antoine were all trading in the west and saw military service in th Ohio valley alongside France's indian allies. A fourth brother Francois (qv) handled the Montréal end of the business.

After the conquest, he refused to swear allegiance and was prevented from touring the western posts to collect his debts before returning to Montréal. He was arrested and temporarily incarcerated in Detroit on a groundless charge of plotting with the Indians against the British occupying forces. His brother Francois having managed to transfer the family trading connections from France to London, Jacques returned to Detroit, his base of operations in 1762. He had presumably by then taken the oath of allegiance.

He supplied the British garrison during Pontiac's uprising and then joined it. He became acting commissary of indian affairs in 1779.

The Baby family lost considerable sums in the discredit of Canada's paper money after the conquest and in the changes in the fur trade (see notes on Francois). But Jacques survived and prospered. He was undoubtedly a vigorous trader, admired by the Indians. He diversified his economic holding by receiving land grants from Indian tribes, in addition to purchases. By 1789, he had 1440 acres of developed land with two waer mills on the American side of the Detroit river and 720 acres on the British side. He also had an immense timber reserve NW of Lake St Clair given him by the Ojibwas.


At his death, he was 58, one-eyed, worn down by the rigours of frontier life, but not an old man. His correspondence and actions suggest that he was straightforward, impulsive, and marked by the stubborn tenacity of the self-made man. Certainly his contemporaries admired him. "Poor Baby dies at Detroit about the first of August, universally regretted", wrote the fur trader John Richardson. "He has not left such a Frenchman behind him."

[Casgrain] ses descendants sont surtout en Ontario. Jacques et Suzanne se sont mariés 6 jours avant la reddition de Détroit aux Anglais. Pontiac était un ami de Baby. Baby avait de nombreux domestiques, quelques serviteurs blancs, et une vingtaine d'esclaves nègres, mulâtres et panis. En 1800, onze ans après sa mort, il restait encore dix de ces esclaves. Au mariage de chacune de ses filles, leur mère avait donne un esclave. L'émancipation eut lieu en 1803.

Duperron aurait eu une fille naturelle, métis indienne, qui aurait épousé "Blue Jacket", chef Huron. Blue Jacket a contribué à la défaite de St.Clair à la Rivière des Miamis 1791 et à celle de Winchester à Muskegon en 1813.
