Notes for Simon Pierre Denys De Bonaventure
[Jette] administrateur de l'Acadie 1704-6; commandant de l'Acadie 1707-10.

[DBC] He began his career in the colonial regular troops and was employed by the Compagnie du Nord and directed a fishery, established by his father, at Ile Percée. In 1690 the establishments there were destroyed by the English. That year, Bonaventure, in command of the "St-Francois-Xavier" accompanied Pierre Lemoyne d'Iberville (qv) to attack Fort York in Hudson Bay. The expedition failed to take Ft York, but threatened the New Severn post which was burned down by its commander Thomas Welsh. From 1692-5, Bonaventure, often with Iberville, sailed between France, Acadia, New ENgland, and Québec, carrying supplies and fighting the English. In 1696, Bonaventure, Iberville, and Jean Vincent d'Abbadie de St-Castin captured and destroyed Fort William Henry at Pemaquid. In 1701 he was appointed second in command in Acadia and King's lieutenant at Port Royal in 1702. In 1705, he petitioned for the post of governor of Acadia, but the appointment was denied him, owing to reports of his liaison with the widowed Madame Louise Damours de Freneuse. In 1707 he was ill in bed while the English under Col March laid siege to Port Royal and destroyed houses, including Bonaventure's. They withdrew after Bernard de St-Castin arrived with a band of Abenakis. The English finally took Port Royal in 1710 and Bonaventure was transported to La Rochelle. He died in Rochefort in 1711.

Bonaventure was accused not only of an affair with Madame de Freneuse but also of trading on his own account with the colonists in Acadia, and worse, with the English. It was also alleged that he was malingering during the siege of 1707. His association with Madame de Freneuse cost him his promotion, but it is not apparent that the French authorities took the other allegations seriously. He may have been named Chevalier de St-Louis. He seems to have been an active and likeable man, possibly not overburdened with moral scruples, and a commander who carried out his duties capably and vigorously.
