Notes for Nicolas Denis
[Jette] arrive en Acadie 1632; gouverneur de l'Acadie 1653; retour en France 1670.

[DBC] Probably a descendant of Jean Denys, the famous explorer who is said to have made expeditions manned by unnamed Bretons as early as 1504, he stated that he belonged to "a family of engineers". Over the course of his long life, he was responsible for the establishment of several fishing and trading posts in Acadia at Miscou, Miramichi, Nipisiguit (now Bathurst NB), St-Pierre, and Chedabuctou (now Guysborough NB), but was ruined several times through rivalries with other traders, political intrigue, wars, and fires. At one point (1653) he owned rights to the coast of the islands of the Gulf of St. Lawrence from Cap Canso to Cap des Rosiers on the Gaspé, including Cape Breton, the Iles de la Madeleine, and Prince Edward Island. But he lost everything and in 1685 was said to be living in beggary in Paris. He returned that year to Acadia and died in 1688 at the age of 89.

Denys's most lasting contribution was as the author of "Description géographique et historique des costes de l'Amérique septentrionale: avec l'histoire du pays", in two volumes, published in France in 1672. Volume I is devoted to a description of the coastal area of Acadia from the Penobscot to Gaspé and to some of his experiences in the region. Volume II deals with fish and fishing and the Micmac Indians among whom he had lived for 40 years. Poorly written, indicating both the author's lack of literary skills and poor education, still this book is entertaining and of great value. He provides an invaluable picture of the country and its people as well as of his own activities and those of such contemporaries as Razilly, d'Aulnay, La Tour, and Le Borgne. His description of the cod fishery, for instance, has been termed the most complete and authoritative available. Those sections that deal with the other natural resources are, for the most part, remarkably enthusiastic and reflect his unquenchable optimism for his country. When we come to the Indians, he supplies the most satisfactory description that had yet appeared. In all, the book is clearly the product of the man of action, one who has thorough knowledge of his subject but who lacks the fluency of style necessary to capture the attention of a large body of the reading public.

Denys's failure was no worse than that of a half dozen of his contemporaries, amond them his rivals Le Borgne and d'Aulnay. Had he lived in another era, he might well have succeeded.
