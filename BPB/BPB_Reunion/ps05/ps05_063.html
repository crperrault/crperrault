774Sir Charles’ run for the Conservatives in Huntingdon in 1882 was supported by Hugh Graham’s Star. The main paper of the area was Sellar’s Huntingdon Gleaner, that had opposed Sir John A Macdonald’s protectionist trade policies since Confederation. Huntingdon’s best times had been in the years of free trade with the US prior to 1866. Sir Charles was soundly defeated and lost his deposit. The Gleaner accused the Star of being the hired organ of the Conservative Party. Within a week of the election, Sellar received the first in a series of letters threatening him with a criminal lawsuit unless he retracted his allegations against the Star. He was sued for libel and hauled three times during the Fall and winter into the city only to find the lawyer for the prosecution (who turned out to be none other than Sir Charles), unprepared to proceed. The case finally came to trial on 14 June 1883, the jury containing, Sellar observed “at least three Catholics and several violent Conservatives”. He would probably have been convicted had his lawyer not managed to have the case dismissed on the technicality that the prosecution had neglected to establish that the prisonner at the bar was the same Robert Sellar as named in the indictment.
