son of a “well-known and worthy Bristol tradesman, James G, who came from a Shropshire family. Educated at Sidcot near Weston-super-mare. Father had been in the drapery and hosiery trade since about 1808 establishing a warehouse at no 12, Bridge St, selling woolen drapery, blankets, a material called “mil puff” and flannel. Moved to corner of Redcliffe St. Campaned to end monopoly of East India Co in 1829. Supported creation of a Bristol General cemetery (director till 1842) and St Peter’s hospital. Active in parish affairs at St Peter. Died 1855. Five sisters: Jane (eldest b 1811) m Jasper Capper in 1835; Mary Ann d after long illness at 25 (memoir later published by Charles); Anna Rebecca b 1829 m John Whiting and founded orphan home in Headlingley; Elizabeth published two books anonymously and ran a school with sister Sophia; Helen became missionary in Antananarivo in Madagascar in late 1860s where she educated girls and collected ferns. Surviving brothers Henry, published two books of juvenile poetry 1838-9; James Bernard became bookseller in Dublin, Joseph Sturge b 1825 became share broker in Nottingham.

[Find obit for Mary Gilpin in Bristol 1841]

Charles was commercial traveller for manuf firm in Manchester (Henry also worked in Manchester). Involved in Beaconite schism, editing Jos Crosfield’s report on n the crisis of the “Manchester episode”, leading to secession of “Evangelical Friends” who laid more emphasis on the Lord’s Supper and baptism by water than on Inner Light. Gilpin was on the orthodox side. Joined father’s firm “James Gilpin and Son” but dissolved in 1840. James Bernard G instead took over the firm and father retired in 1846. Charles m Anna Crouch (day of Wm, flour merchant of Falmouth) at Friends Meeting House in that town.

Successful in book race, at 5 Bishopsgate St Without in London, after being in partnership w Jos Smith in Bedford St. Close to Gracechurch Mtg , to Broad St where anti-slavery and other philanthropic societies were based, ad near to Quaker suburbs of Stoke Newington and Tottenham.

Printed Quaker and social or moral reform titles, anti-slavery, temperance, medical. Turned down Uncle Tom’s Cabin. Owner of “The Friend”. Retired in 1853.
