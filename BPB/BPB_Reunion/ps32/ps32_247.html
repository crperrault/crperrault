Notes for Thomas Crewdson
331p 66

337 [p122] He was a hosier in the town and was one of the three partners who opened on January 1, 1778 what was later to be known as Crewdson's Bank, as distinct from Wakefield’s Bank, opened on the same day. The other two partners were Joseph Maude (d 1803) and Christopher Wilson (1731-1804). Maude brought to the concern considerable knowledge of the Northeast, including its banking traditions, and had other fairly widespread valuable links, while Christopher Wilson had local knowledge, as Crewdson also had, but had the advantages of being a member of the established church, which may well have attracted customers who might not have patronized a purely Quaker bank. Maude was perhaps the most prominent of the founders, and the major Crewdson involvement was left to Thomas's son and grandson. But he saw the bank pull through the 1793 crisis and deserves credit for starting what was to be a long family association.
