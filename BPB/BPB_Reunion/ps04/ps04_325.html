Notes for Louis Coulon De Villiers
Capitaine. Surnommé "le grand Villiers", qui battit Washington au Fort Necessity (1754) et vengea la mort de son frère Joseph, sieur de Jumonville. Sa femme, MA Prudhomme, était l'arrière petite fille de Blaise Juillet, compagnon de Dollard.

[DBC] Louis Coulon was a captain in 1754 when Gov. Duquesne sent him and 700 men to reinforce a garrison at forts between Lake Erie and the Ohio RIver. When he arrived at Fort Duquesne (Pittsburgh) on 26 June 1754, he learned that although France and England were not at war, a party of 30 Canadians led by his brother Jumonville had been ambushed by a detachment from Virginia led by George Washington (and sent by the governor of Virginia, Robert Dinwiddie qv) . Jumonville and nine other Canadians had been killed. Coulon was granted command of a detachment of 500 men to avenge this act and drive the Americans off the territory claimed by France.

En route, they came upon the site of the ambush of Jumonville's party, where Washington had left the scalped corpses unburied, a prey to wolves and crows. On 3 July, the Canadians made contact with the enemy at Fort Necessity (Farmington PA). For 9 hours, between rain squalls, the Canadian forces fired on the enemy inflicting a 100 casualties (about 1/4 of the American forces) with only 3 killed and 17 wounded. By nightfall, with his men exhausted and powder running low, Coulon offered to parley. Washington agreed. The Americans were permitted to return to their country in safety, under the condition that they abandon their establishments west of the Alleghanies for the next 12 months and relinguqish their prisoners. They handed over two hostages, Robert Stobo and Jacob Can Braan.

The next day, the Americans fled so precipatedly that Washington left his journal behind, which the French made use of, to label the English admitted murderers and assassins. The Americans subsequently made plain that they had no intention of honoring the settlement. French prisoners were not released, Stobo violated his parole and acted as a spy and before the end of the year, Washington accompanied Braddock in a full-scale assault on Fort Duquesne.

Louis Coulon subsequently distinguished himself in the capture of Forts Oswego and William Henry in 1755. He was made Chevalier de St- Louis, award he received days before his death from smallpox.
