[DBC] Businessman, militia officer, politician and JP. A founder of canadian banking.

His father was s shopkeeper and baker in Québec, who died when Augustin, the eldest of 7 children, was 10. College Saint-Raphael in Mtl 1794.

He entered the auction business in Mtl as an employee of Henry Richard Symes and eventually took over he business in1802. Later partnerships with Thomas Aylwin and Thomas Harkness. The second partnership failed in 1806, but auctioneering (import wholesale of dry goods and sale in large lots to local buyers) would continue to be central to AC's career. He developed a reputation in the world of British colonial commerce and finance. From the turn of the century, he adopted Austin as an abbreviated and anglicized version of his given name, at first in English then in French.

By 1807 he was in possession of auction rooms on Rue Notre-Dame, next to James McGill's, but he remained mired in financial difficulties leading to seizures of property in 1808-1813. In 1811 he formed a company under his wife's name (Mary C. Cuvillier & Co.)

During the War of 1812, he served in the 5th Select Embodied Militia Batallion of LC (the "Devil's Own"), initially as lt. and adj. In 1813, under cover to find deserters from the militia, he obtained intelligence about American forces in the Salmon River area where he was known as a merchant. By 4-1814 he was a capt. in the Chasseurs Canadiens but resigned a month later after being bypassed for promotion. In recognition of his services he was granted a medal with Chateauguay clasp and 800 acres of land in Litchfield Twp. In 3-1815, his properties were again seized by the sheriff. In 1816, he surprised two robbers in his store and held them there, with the assistance of his neighbor Joseph Masson until the soldiers arrived.

He first ran for election in Huntingdon in 1809 and was finally elected in 1814 and quickly became a rising star in the Parti Canadien where his knowledge of the local economy made him indispensable in debates on the control of govmt finances.

His presence in the councils of the Parti mitigated the business community's hostility. He became the driving force in the movement to incorporate a colonial bank when, after the war, the redemption of the army's bills threatened to leave the colony without paper money or sufficient specie. His efforts were frustrated by several prorogations of the legislature, and led to his participation in the founding of the Bank of Montréal in 1822, led by John Richardson. In 1818 he was one of the 9 founding shareholders in the Montréal Fire Insurance Company, and became Pres in 1820.

In 1817, he attacked judge Louis-Charles Foucher for partiality and conflict of interest in a decision adverse to him on a business dispute. The legislature, which had already impeached Jonathan Sewell and James Monk, did so for Foucher but the adjudication bogged down in politics.

In 1820 he proposed a law to remunerate members of the Assembly to open the house to members other than from the liberal professions, but Papineau opposed the bill as too democratic.

During the great debates over the colony's finances, he scrutinized executive accounts while Papineau and John Neilson argued principles. In 1821, he was one of 4 commissioners named to conduct negotiations with Upper Canada on the division of customs duties. Throughout the 1820s, AC and Neilson preached fiscal responsibility on the part of the assembly and denounced what they considered its tendency to excessive appropriations for non-administrative expenses, such as public works.

In 1822-3, he was active (with Joseph Masson) in the Canadian party's campaign against union of the Canadas. He and Masson benefited from their association with the PC, as Canadian merchants often preferred to get their merchandise through them than through British houses. Nonetheless, he had financial difficulties in 1823 and again in 1825- 6, which led to his breaking his business relationship with the Bank of Montréal.

In 1824-25, in GOv Dalhousie's absence, he chaired the budget committee that worked out with Lt Gov Burton a compromise supply bill that became a model for its future demands. When Dalhousie rejected Papineau as speaker in 11-1827, it was "the bankrupt auctioneer", as Dalhousie called him, who moved the house's denunciation of the action. He was named to the committee (with Neilson and Viger) that took a petition to London complaining of Dalhousie's administration. During his absence, his business and family were confided to Masson. In London, he gave lengthy testimony on political and economic issues, argued in favor of the seigneurial system, French civil law, an elected legislative council, and an independent judiciary. He opposed the establishment of a colonial aristocracy and any change of the 1791 constitution without the consent of both parties. The Canada committee's report responded favourably to most of the delegates' demands, and completely on financial issues.

After his return to Canada he hoped that he would be appointed as the colony's agent in London which he found a businessman's paradise. But the financial question having been settled in favour of the assembly, it was thought, Papineau turned the attention of the Patriote Party (founded in 1826) to more strictly political objectives, such as the elected legislative council. He thus preferred Viger as agent and a confrontation with AC in early 1829 led to to the beginning of his estrangement from the party. This was increased by AC's defense in 1830 of the Bank of Montréal and its charter. But Papineau noted in 3-1830 that a froup of deputies was coalescing around AC.

He was returned for Laprairie in 1830, but his alienation from the party continued as Viger was finally voted as London agent in 3-1831, with only a few members from the townships and 4 or 5 canadians supporting AC. He confronted the party again later that year arguing that a proposed baord of audit should be staffed by professional auditors, not political appointees. With Neilson, Mondelet and other moderates, he opposed the "fabrique" bill proposed by Bourdages in late 1831.

He was appointed JP in 1830.

The rift appeared outside the asembly as well. He was notably absent from the group led by Fabre to form the Maison Canadienne de Commerce in1832. He backed the govmt candidate, Stanley Bagg, in a Mtl by election in 1832. When a riot broke out at a poll, he was one of the magistrates considered responsible for summoning the tropps to fire on the mob; 3 canadiens were killed and AC was vilified as a "bureaucrat" by Patriote leaders.

In 1833, he denounced in the assembly Patriote demands for an elected council. In 1834, he was one of only 6 Canadians who refused to support the 92 resolutions. He joined an association of adversaries of the Patriotes and supported the continued connection between the UK and Canada. A popular Patriote song, "C'est la faute a Papineau" reviled him as a turncoat. He left poitics at the time of the elections of 1834.

His conflicts with the Patriotes developed at a time when his business fortunes were improving. He had become Mtl's leading auctioneer of imported manufactured goods, fish, salt, and liquors and ofthe stock of bankrupt merchants. In 1836 he renovated his store on Rue St-Paul. He acted as an agent, trustee, and stockbroker. In 1836 he was named Montréal director of the Bank of British North America, founded to act a a colonial bank in London. He expanded in Upper Canada in the 1830s. He was president of the Montréal Committee of Trade 1837-41 and a member of the group that led to its incorporationas the Mtl Board of Trade in 1841-2.

During the rebellion of 1837, he was major and commander of Mtl's 5th Militia Batallion. In 11-1837, he and Turton Penn, as magistrates, signed the requisition for military assistance that enable British troops to march on St-Denis. In 1-1838, he was a founder and VP of the Association Loyale Canadienne du District de Montréal, which denounced the leaders of the rebelliions and the supporters of the union of the Canadas and caled for continuation of the 1791 constitution. With Colborne's approval, he issued paper currency to offset suspension of specie payments to the troops. He suffered a serious etback in 1837-8 after the failure of a Mtl merchant cost him #3000 and provoked a run on his bills, but he survived.

FOllowing Durham's report, he, Neilson and DB Viger opposed union and he was elected in the 1841 election in huntingdon on an anti-union ticket. LH Lafontaine, who stillheld a grudge against AC for his refusal to support the 92 Resolutions, allowed himself to be convinced that he would be a better speaker than one of the more radical candidates, because of his extensive business connections in both provinces, and since he opposed union, the civil list, equal representation of the two provinces, fundingthe debt at the expense of Lower Canada, and the proscription of French inthe legislature. He was elected.

As speaker, he developed exceedingly cordial relations with successive governors. He supported Metcalfe in his battle against Lafontaine and Baldwin over control of patronage, which cost him his Huntingdon seat, andhe was defeated in Rimouski as well. At 65, his public career came to an end.

His auctioneering business, now called Cuvillier an Sons, counted among its clients the Seminaire de St-Sulpice, from which he purchased some lands. He died of typhus in 1849.

He was a rare figure in the history of LC, as an exception to the conflict between the British merchant community and the nationalist parties. He left the party when it radicalized in the late 20s, with Heney, Quesnel, Vallieres de St-Real, Debartzch, and the Mondelet father and sons. Their absence enabled the radical Patriote leaders to push the reform movement ever more rapidly and surely towards disaster.

A portrait of him by Theophile Hamel hangs in the Speakers' Corridor of the House of COmmons. Another protrait is in the Chateau Ramezay.

Fit des études au collège Saint-Raphaël, à Montréal, en 1794.

Entré au service d'un encanteur de Montréal, prit la direction de l'entreprise en 1802. Par la suite, se lança à son propre compte dans la vente à l'encan, particulièrement celle de
marchandises importées en gros et revendues en lots. Fonda, entre autres, la Cuvillier, Aylwin, and Harkness, la M.C. Cuvillier and Company et la Cuvillier and Sons. Lié à la
mise sur pied et à l'administration de la Banque de Montréal (1817) et de la Compagnie d'assurance de Montréal contre les accidents du feu (1818). Dans les années 1830, fut
membre du conseil d'administration de la Banque de l'Amérique septentrionale britannique, de Londres, et président du Committee of Trade de Montréal. Fut syndic et agent de
change. Fit aussi affaire dans le Haut-Canada. Propriétaire foncier.

Défait dans Huntingdon en 1809. Élu député de cette circonscription en 1814; lié au parti canadien. Réélu en 1816, avril 1820, juillet 1820, 1824 et 1827. Fut l'un des trois
délégués du parti patriote envoyés en Angleterre, en 1828, pour présenter des demandes de réformes. S'éloigna du parti patriote à compter de 1829. Élu dans Laprairie en 1830;
n'appuya pas les Quatre-vingt-douze Résolutions. Défait en 1834. S'occupa d'administration municipale, à Montréal, avant 1833, puis entre 1836 et 1840. Servit en qualité
d'officier de milice durant la rébellion de 1837 et fut un des fondateurs de l'Association loyale canadienne du district de Montréal en janvier 1838. Élu dans Huntingdon en 1841;
était antiunioniste; élu orateur de l'Assemblée le 14 juin 1841, le demeura jusqu'au 23 septembre 1844. Défait dans Huntingdon et dans Rimouski en 1844.

Pendant la guerre de 1812, servit dans la milice d'élite et les renseignements. Obtint divers postes de commissaire.

Décédé à Montréal, le 11 juillet 1849, à l'âge de 69 ans et 10 mois. Inhumé dans l'église Notre-Dame, le 12 juillet 1849.

Avait épousé dans la paroisse Notre-Dame de Montréal, le 7 novembre 1802, Claire Perrault, fille de Joseph Perrault et de Marie-Anne Tavernier.

Beau-père d'Alexandre-Maurice Delisle. Beau-frère de Joseph Perrault.

Austin Cuvillier est né à Québec le 20 août 1779 et a été baptisé sous le prénom Augustin. Il est le fils aîné d'Augustin, commerçant au détail, et d'Angélique Miot dit Girard. Il est inscrit au Collège Saint-Raphaël de Montréal en 1794 mais ne semble pas y avoir complété ses études. Austin Cuvillier travaille pour un encanteur montréalais, Henry Richard Symes, puis commence à son compte dans la vente de marchandises importées en gros. Entre 1802 et 1811, il fonde successivement la Cuvillier & Aylwin de Montréal; la Cuvillier, Aylwin, & Harkness de Montréal; la Aylwin, Harkness & Company de Québec; et la M.C. Cuvillier & Co (compagnie fondée initialement sous la raison sociale Mary C. Cuvillier & Company) de Montréal.
Malgré la faillite de son commerce de Québec et certains déboires financiers à Montréal, Cuvillier ne diminue pas ses ardeurs. Il connaît bien les rouages du négoce outre-Atlantique ainsi que ceux des banques et de la finance. Il participe à la création de la Banque de Montréal en 1817 et devient président de la Compagnie d'assurance de Montréal contre les accidents du feu en 1820. Il est aussi membre de conseils d'administration, syndic, agent de change et propriétaire foncier. Ses tâches l'autorisent d'ailleurs à vendre des actions de plusieurs banques du Canada.
Austin Cuvillier conjugue ses fonctions d'administrateur et commerçant à celles de politicien. Lié au parti canadien, il est élu député en 1814 dans la circonscription de Huntingdon et y sera réélu à cinq reprises. En 1828, il est un des délégués envoyé par le parti patriote (nom du parti canadien depuis 1826) en Angleterre pour exiger des réformes et un adoucissement de l'administration Dalhousie. Peu après son retour, il se distance du parti et de Louis-Joseph Papineau. Élu en 1830 dans Laprairie (autrefois Huntingdon), il réfute les Quatre-vingt-douze Résolutions. S'ensuit une guerre ouverte entre Papineau et Cuvillier puis la défaite de celui-ci aux élections de 1834. Cuvillier connaît la victoire dans Laprairie en 1841 puis la défaite en 1844 dans ce même comté ainsi que dans Rimouski contre les réformistes de L.-H. La Fontaine. Ces revers sonnent le glas de la carrière politique de Austin Cuvillier. Il se consacre donc à son commerce d'encan connut sous le nom de la Cuvillier & Sons de Montréal.
Hormis sa carrière politique et commerciale, Austin Cuvillier prend part aussi à la vie militaire. Il est adjudant du 5e bataillon de la milice d'élite pendant la guerre de 1812 et est commandant lors des troubles de 1837. Nommé juge de paix en 1830, on l'accuse d'avoir ordonné la fusillade ayant fait trois morts lors des élections partielles à Montréal en 1832. Il est décédé à Montréal du typhus le 11 juillet 1849 à 69 ans. Il a épousé Marie-Claire Perreault, fille de Joseph et de Marie-Anne Tavernier, à Montréal le 7 novembre 1802. De cette union sont nés sept enfants, dont Marie-Angélique qui a marié Alexandre-Maurice Delisle.
