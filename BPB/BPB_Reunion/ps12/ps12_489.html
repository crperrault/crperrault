Notes for John Ostell
[DBC] Arpenteur, fonctionnaire, architecte et homme d'affaires. Arpenteur municipal; dressa le premier plan complet de Montréal (1840-1). Comme architecte: bureau des douanes de la Place Royale (1836), McGill Arts Bldg (1839-43), palais de Justice (1850-56), clochers de église Notre-Dame (1841-43), église de la Visitation à Sault-au-Récollet (1850), église ND de Grâce (1850), grand séminaire de Montréal (1855-64).

Avec Albert Furniss, construit la première usine a gaz de Montréal (1836-38). Avec William Molson, chemin de fer de Montréal et Champlain, ch. de fer de Montréal et New York (1857), ch de fer de Montréal (omnibus) (1861).

Milita en faveur de l'annexion aux États Unis. Anglican marié à une catholique, converti au catholicisme en 1891. 8 enfants.

[The Gazette 141115]
It was something of a scoop. On Nov. 12, 1835, The Gazette and La Minerve announced the results of a competition to build a new custom house in the city. The stories named the winning architect, described the proposed building and included the site where it would be built.
What made the story a scoop was that the final decision had still not been made public. Not until nearly a month later, on Dec. 5, did the House of Assembly strike a committee to assess three competing plans. An Assembly resolution to pay for the structure would not come until February, and only in March did legislation provide for its actual construction.
The two newspapers still had most of it right. The architect would be John Ostell, “our fellow citizen” as The Gazette described him. The building site, facing the harbour, would either be beside the old Ste-Anne market or nearby on a vacant lot on Rue de la Commune.
To be sure, the newspapers slipped up with the overall shape of the proposed building. La Minerve described “un centre et deux ailes,” and The Gazette went further, noting the centre section would be recessed between the flanking wings. But in the end, the two wings were never built because the eventual site, on the old marketplace, was too small.
Nevertheless, it seems clear that the House of Assembly, in December and on into the following March, was merely rubber-stamping decisions that had already been made. This is all the more remarkable considering that Ostell was virtually an unknown quantity. He was just 22 years old and had landed in Montreal scarcely a year before.
He had trained as an architect and land surveyor in his native England. Once in Montreal, he apprenticed himself to a local surveyor, the better to acquaint himself with the methods based on French principles in use here.
While the two unsuccessful architects for the commission are not known, there’s no reason to suspect they were so incompetent that Ostell won by default. It quickly became clear, from his custom house and from the many buildings that followed, that he was exceptionally well grounded in the classicism of earlier English architects like James Gibb and, above all, Christopher Wren.
Such sophistication had tremendous appeal for the custom house committee. The opening of the Lachine Canal in 1825 had brought an economic boom, and the Montreal Harbour Commission was established five years later to oversee improvements to the increasingly busy port. Montreal was being tied more tightly to trade with Europe, and a suitable home was needed for the expanded bureaucracy — customs collectors, landing master and so on — that this entailed.
By 1838, when the custom house was completed, Ostell was on a roll. That year, he designed a building, today’s Arts Building, for McGill College. An orphanage, the old courthouse (now known as the Édifice Lucien Saulnier) and an expansion of Montreal’s jail duly followed.
He also won ecclesiastical commissions. A number of notable Montreal churches, including Notre Dame de Grâce in the west end and the now-demolished St-Ann’s in Griffintown, are his. So are the two towers of Notre Dame Basilica that loom over Place d’Armes.
He also designed several houses, commercial buildings and factories. In all, he was responsible for more than two dozen buildings in Montreal. Without a doubt, he was the leading English-speaking architect of the day.
And through it all, he somehow had time for a parallel career in business. As early as 1836, he was involved in producing gas for indoor lamps and street lighting. He speculated in industrial land along the Lachine Canal and opened a lumber mill there. From 1859 to 1865, he was president of the Montreal and Champlain, founded in 1836 as Canada’s first railroad. He was a director of the Royal Insurance Company.
The old custom house, like many of Ostell’s buildings that survive, today has a new vocation. It is now part of the Pointe à Callière museum of history and archeology.
