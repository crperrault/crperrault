Notes for Dr Antonius Yusuf Manasseh
Anotnios Manasseh was born in Lebanon and graduated from American University in Beirut as a pharmacist in 1895. He then worked with his brother, Dr Beshara Manasseh. Dr. Beshara had graduated from AUB’s first class of medical doctors in 1877 and met Theophilos Waldmeier (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophilus_Waldmeier) who was a missionary who had fled Abysynia to Levant (Lebanon). Waldmeier was meeting Cybil & Eli Jones from Maine who had come to establish Quaker presence in the rush of missionaries in the 1870s. Dr. Beshara became a Friend and took his brothers Antonios and Spiridon to Brummana where he founded the first Hospital and Antonios and Spiridon went through high school (Waldmeir had founded Brummana High School, which still exists (http://www.bhs.edu.lb/)).

Hettie and Antonios must have met in UK when Antonios was doing his medical studies at Leeds University, from which he graduated in 1905. They were likely married in Lebanon ca 1909 and had two children, George Antonius and Dorothy in 1910-11. Hettie, Dorothy and George returned to London at the onset of WW I as the Turks were on the side of the Axis. Antonios stayed in Lebanon and practiced medicine till his death in 1929, making frequent visits to London to see his family.

He was called Tanius, and is mentioned as such in a letter from John T Dorland to Spiridon Manasseh of 1895. (see John To Dorland, by William King Baker, p 282)

[British Medical Journal, Aug 15, 1931] A short but detailed and attractive biography of Dr. Antonius Manasseh has now been prepared by C. G. NAISH, principal of the Brummana High School in the Lebanon; for the many friends in this country and in Syria of a medical pioneer who won high esteem as superintendent of the medical work of the Friends Foreign Mlssion -Association, and in connexion with the Syrian and Palestine Relief Fund. He was once described in the British Medical Journal as " a bridge between individuals and peoples who misunderstood one another." Dr. Manasseh was largely concerned-with the introduction of modern medicine into a country where the age of magic was still continuing. The biography indicates how he was able to achieve his great success; it will be welcomed by many who had no previous knowledge of a very humble and most energetic member of the British Medical Association, who was equally at home in the East and in the West.
