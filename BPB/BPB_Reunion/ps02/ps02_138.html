Notes for Arthur Benington
See Edward Benington's notes of 1978.

Sent to Canada to open a branch of his father's tea business, British
and Benington. The operation lost $100,000 in the first two years,
and he was fired by his father. He became a journalist, being, among
other things, the science editor of the New York World. His hobby
was Dante, and his knowledge of italian was good enough for him to
get the job of heading the US Information Agency in Italy during WWI.
Made Commander of the Crown of Italy by Victor Emmanuel. Made
Knight Commander of the Order of Danilo by the Queen of Montenegro.
He was then recalled from Montenegro by Woodrow Wilson for impolitic
remarks. Born a Quaker, he converted to Catholicism and was active
in that church. He helped Bishop Molloy choose over 100 stained
glass windows for the church of Queen of All Saints, Brooklyn.
President of National Catholic Writers Guild.

Harold Benington says that Arthur "broke away from the Quaker Sect
shortly after coming to the United States and became a Roman
Catholic, in which faith his four sons were brought up".

329 [Obit, NYT] Arthur Benington Dies.
Authority on Italy was member of the Staff of The World

Arthur Benington, Assistant Sunday Editor of the World since 1904, and an authority on Italian affairs, died yesterday at his home, 86 St. james Place, Brooklyn. He was 59 year old.

Mr Benington first came to this country in 1883 as manager of the Toronto branch of his father’s tea business. He began newspaper work as a reporter on The Duluth Tribune in 1887. For five years in the 90’s he was on the staff of The New York Journal. On several occasions he visited Italy before the war and his extensive knowledge of the language an d the people caused him to be sent to that country as representative of the Committee on Public Information. He was President of the Catholic Writers’ Guild, Vice President of the Dante Alighieri Society and a member of the National Dante Committee. He received the Orders of the Crown of Italy and of Saints Maurice and Lazarus.

[From Wikipedia article on Bootham]
Bootham did not set out to cultivate a progressive image but offered a ‘whole school’ approach distinctly in advance of the education offered by more prestigious nineteenth century public schools, where there had been a transition from ‘godliness and classical learning’ to ‘manliness and games’. Quaker teachers were often trained at the Flounders Institute at Ackworth and sometimes took a London external degree while teaching. Many had a keen interest in natural history which was enthusiastically shared by the pupils and led to a serious interest in science at the school which went on to produce a number of distinguished scientists in many areas.
This scientific interest was in keeping with the intellectual developments in the city of York which in 1822 had formed the Yorkshire Philosophical Society (YPS). In 1853 Bootham became one of the first schools to have its own observatory, equipped with a refracting telescope manufactured by the notable York instrument maker Thomas Cooke.
Quakers stressed the importance of a constructive use of leisure time. Many boys produced impressive essays and classified collections. Some, such as Silvanus P. Thompson (Bootham 1858-67) became eminent in their field – he was a professor of science and worked with Michael Faraday on electromagnetism. In the late nineteenth century many of the Rowntree family sons were educated at Bootham, one of them, Arthur Rowntree, becoming Headmaster (1899–1927).
